A camper trailer is the other half of a great South African overland setup. If you already own a [Land Cruiser](/listings/) — or you're [renting a fully-equipped one](/useful-info/land-cruiser-rental-south-africa/) — towing a kitted trailer or off-road caravan behind it gives you a proper basecamp without the cost of buying one outright. And if you only need it for a trip or two a year, renting makes far more sense than owning.

This guide covers the two ways to rent a camper trailer in South Africa, what it actually costs, the towing and licence rules that catch people out, and exactly what to check before you book.

> **Quick answer:** There are two ways to rent a camper trailer in South Africa — **peer-to-peer** (renting a private owner's trailer through Kampi, from around **R519/day**, the widest choice but variable quality) or from a **fleet specialist** such as Conqueror, Kubu 4x4, Go Camp, CampEzi or Bundu (professionally maintained and fully kitted, roughly **R600–R1,500/day**). Most off-road trailers and caravans need a **Code EB licence** to tow legally (anything over 750 kg GVM), though any modern Land Cruiser has more than enough muscle for it. Always confirm the **insurance scope** and a **breakdown plan** before you pay.

## The two ways to rent a camper trailer

This is the single most important thing to understand, because it shapes everything else — price, choice, quality and risk.

### 1. Peer-to-peer (renting a private owner's trailer)

**Kampi** ([kampi.co.za](https://kampi.co.za/)) is South Africa's peer-to-peer camper and caravan rental marketplace — essentially "Airbnb for caravans." You rent a trailer directly from its private owner. The platform handles the booking, payment (held in escrow), insurance and verification.

- **Upside:** the most choice and usually the best prices — lightweight trailers start from around **R519/day**. There are hundreds of listings across all nine provinces, and many owners include their own kit and tips.
- **Downside:** because every trailer belongs to a different private owner, **condition, kit and responsiveness vary from listing to listing.** The platform mediates disputes but, in its own words, "cannot directly control individual rental agreements." This shows in the reviews: Kampi rates around **4.8★ on Google** (120+ reviews) but is more mixed on **Trustpilot and Hellopeter (roughly 3 / 5)**. The pattern is consistent — most trips go smoothly, but the weak point is the edge cases: a breakdown far from home, a grey area in what the insurance covers, or a slow deposit refund. So with P2P, **reading the specific listing's reviews and asking the owner direct questions matters more than usual** (more on that below).

### 2. Fleet specialists (renting from a professional rental company)

These companies own and maintain their own fleet of off-road trailers and caravans. You're not dealing with a private individual — you're dealing with a business whose whole job is keeping rental trailers trip-ready.

- **Upside:** consistent, professionally maintained trailers, full overland kit, a proper handover, and branch/roadside support if something goes wrong.
- **Downside:** higher day rates and less variety than the P2P pool.

The best-known specialists include:

- **[Conqueror](https://conqueror.co.za/rentals/)** — the premium name in off-road camper trailers and caravans. A 30-year industry leader whose units have won *Caravan of the Year* and *Off-Road Trailer of the Year*; they manufacture their own trailers and rent them out fully equipped.
- **[Kubu 4x4](https://kubu4x4.co.za/)** — the biggest specialist fleet, with five branches (Krugersdorp, Brackenfell, Potchefstroom, Bethlehem and Knysna), renting Conqueror, Echo and Bush Lapa units. Rated **4.7★ on Google** and consistently well-regarded by the overlanding community for fair pricing and hassle-free handovers.
- **[Go Camp](https://www.gocamp.co.za/trailer-rentals)** — off-road trailers built for harsh conditions, fitted with range-topping camping gear; branches in Cape Town and Johannesburg, with both Code B and Code EB options and a 3-night minimum. Its Gauteng branch is one of the best-rated in the country at **4.9★ across 340+ Google reviews**.
- **[CampEzi](https://campezi.co.za/)** (Pretoria) — 20 years of overlanding experience, renting Echo and Conqueror off-road trailers (the lightweight Echo 3 even tows on a Code B licence). Trailers are inspected and repacked before every trip, and they're well-reviewed on the 4x4 forums.
- **[Bundu 4x4 Caravan & Trailer Hire](https://www.bunducaravanhire.co.za/)** — off-road bush caravans, trailers and rooftop tents, roughly **R750–R980/day**, with cross-border travel allowed.
- **[Camp Guru](https://www.campguru.co.za/)** — budget to late-model luxury caravans in Gauteng (Edenvale). Note: **no cross-border** trailer rentals, and a Code EB licence is required for caravans over 750 kg.

> **A note on reviews and ratings.** Most of these operators are **strongly rated on Google** — Go Camp's Gauteng branch sits at 4.9★ (340+ reviews) and Kubu 4x4 at 4.7★, while Kampi is around 4.8★. Two cautions, though: Kampi is more mixed on Trustpilot and Hellopeter (roughly 3/5), where unhappy edge-case customers tend to land; and a brand like Conqueror also *manufactures and sells* caravans, so its general review pages mix in warranty and sales complaints rather than reflecting the rental arm specifically. Best practice: **read the recent Google reviews for the exact branch you'd book from**, and weigh first-hand 4x4-community feedback alongside the star number.

> **Which should you choose?** Rent **peer-to-peer (Kampi)** if you want the widest choice and the best price and you're comfortable vetting an individual listing. Rent from a **fleet specialist** if you'd rather pay more for guaranteed condition and proper support — especially for a long or remote trip where a breakdown would ruin the holiday.

## Can your vehicle tow it? (The part people get wrong)

Renting a *trailer* is different from renting a whole vehicle: you supply the tow vehicle. So before anything else, make sure your vehicle and your licence are legal for the trailer you want.

**Licence — the most common gotcha.** A standard **Code B** licence only covers towing a trailer with a gross vehicle mass (GVM) up to **750 kg**. Most off-road caravans and larger camper trailers are heavier than that, which means you legally need a **Code EB** licence. Towing on the wrong licence is illegal **and voids your insurance** — so check the trailer's GVM against your licence first.

**Towing capacity — rarely the problem on a Land Cruiser.** Most modern Land Cruisers are rated to tow roughly **2,500–3,500 kg braked**, comfortably more than almost any camper trailer (typically 750–1,500 kg loaded). Still, check two numbers in your owner's manual:

- **Braked towing capacity** — the maximum trailer weight your vehicle can pull.
- **Gross Combination Mass (GCM)** — the maximum *combined* weight of your fully-loaded vehicle *and* trailer. It's easy to be under your tow rating but over GCM once both are packed.

**Brakes and tow bar.** Trailers over 750 kg should have **over-run (braked) couplings** — confirm the trailer has them. Your **tow bar and tow ball** must be rated for the loaded trailer weight, with roughly **7–10% of the trailer's weight pushing down on the tow ball** (the "nose weight") for stable towing.

**Fuel.** Plan for it: towing increases fuel consumption by roughly **25–30%**, which matters a lot on long Kalahari or cross-border legs where fill-ups are far apart.

## What to check before you book

The universal rental checks — insurance excess, how the deposit is held, mileage limits, fuel policy and a full photo walkaround at collection — are the same as for any rental, and we cover them in detail in our [Land Cruiser rental guide](/useful-info/land-cruiser-rental-south-africa/). Here are the checks **specific to a camper trailer**, drawn from where real rentals go wrong:

- **GVM vs your licence and tow bar.** Get the exact loaded GVM in writing and match it to your Code B/EB licence and tow bar rating.
- **Over-run brakes and a breakaway cable** — present and working.
- **Tyres, wheel bearings and the spare.** Ask when bearings were last repacked and tyres replaced — bearings are the classic trailer failure on long gravel. Confirm the spare and jack fit the *trailer*, not just your car.
- **Exactly what kit is included** — tent, bedding, kitchen, fridge/cooler, gas, water tanks, solar/battery, awning. Don't assume; get a list.
- **Insurance scope, not just the daily price.** Confirm in writing what the cover *excludes* (gravel-road damage, tyres, water damage, cross-border) and what the excess is. "Insured" and "covered for what you're about to do" are not the same thing.
- **The breakdown plan.** This is the big one, especially peer-to-peer: *if it breaks down 400 km from anywhere, who fixes it, who pays, and how fast?* A good owner or company has a clear, confident answer. A vague one is a red flag — get it resolved before you pay.
- **Cross-border letters.** Heading into Botswana, Namibia or beyond? You'll need the owner's authorisation letter and the trailer's papers. Confirm these exist before you book.

Whichever route you choose, the cheapest option isn't always the best value: on a long or remote trip, a slightly pricier, well-maintained trailer with real support behind it can be the difference between a great holiday and a stranded one.

## Planning the trip

![A camper trailer set up with awning, camp table, chairs and ground mat for a shakedown before a trip](https://pub-6c900fb2e73a4b89bc049099101e4591.r2.dev/images/posts/camper-trailer-awning-camp-setup.jpg)

Once the trailer's sorted, the rest is route and prep:

- Use [Tracks4Africa](https://tracks4africa.co.za/) for off-road routing, and keep paper maps as backup — phone coverage is sparse in remote areas.
- Do a **shakedown night** close to home before any remote expedition, so you learn the trailer's quirks where it's easy to fix things.
- Reduce tyre pressure on loose surfaces — around **1.8 bar for gravel and 1.4 bar for sand** — and slow right down over corrugations to protect your (and the trailer's) wheel bearings.
- Pack enough water in reserve for the whole group for two to three days.

## The bottom line

Renting a camper trailer is the cheapest, lowest-commitment way to get a proper overland setup behind your Land Cruiser. **Go peer-to-peer with Kampi** for the widest choice and best prices if you're happy to vet the individual listing, or **go with a fleet specialist** like Conqueror, Kubu 4x4 or Go Camp if you'd rather pay for guaranteed condition and support. Either way, sort the licence and towing maths first, pin down the insurance scope and the breakdown plan, and you're set.
