A classic Land Cruiser in South Africa currently carries a **median asking price of about R480,000**, based on the live listings tracked on landcruisersa.co.za. Asking prices run from roughly **R250,000** for honest, driveable examples to **R999,000** for the best restored or exceptionally original trucks. The spread is the story: with classics, the year on the licence disc tells you almost nothing — **condition, originality and paperwork set the price**. This guide covers what the chassis codes mean, what each era is worth, and what to check before you pay. All figures are **asking prices, updated daily** on the [Classic Cruisers page](/classics/) — not final transaction prices, and classics in South Africa routinely sell below asking.

## What counts as a classic Land Cruiser?

The classics are the **chassis-code era** — the trucks badged by code rather than by series number on the tailgate:

- **40 Series (1960–1984)** — the icon. The **FJ40** is the short-wheelbase soft-top/hard-top; the **FJ45** is the long-wheelbase pickup that worked half the farms in South Africa; the **HJ45** is its diesel twin. This is the most commonly listed classic Cruiser in SA.
- **55 Series (1967–1980)** — the "Iron Pig" station wagon, the first family Land Cruiser. Rare in South Africa; a good one almost never comes up.
- **60 Series (1980–1990)** — the last of the classics. The **FJ60** and fuel-injected **FJ62** petrol wagons and the **HJ60/61** diesels are the bridge between the farm truck and the modern family 4x4, and the cheapest way into a classic Cruiser today.

Decoding the badge is simple once you know the pattern: the first letter is the engine (**F** = petrol straight-six; **B** and **H** = diesel), **J** means Land Cruiser, the first number is the series, and the second digit indicates the body or wheelbase. For the full lineage from the 1951 Toyota BJ onwards, see our [Land Cruiser history](/useful-info/land-cruiser-history/).

## Why prices have climbed

The global collector market for 40 and 60 Series Cruisers has grown sharply over the past decade, and South Africa sits on one of the deeper pools of survivors — decades of farm and fleet use meant thousands of FJ45s and HJ45s sold new here. The good ones are getting harder to find: rust, hard use and neglect have thinned the herd, and the best examples increasingly go to collectors rather than back to work. That is the appreciation story — but it belongs to **documented, honest trucks**, not to projects. A rough truck is a labour of love, not an investment.

## What to check before you buy

1. **Rust, everywhere it hides.** Windscreen frame and gutters first — both are notorious. Then sills, rear quarters, the load-bed supports on a 45, and the chassis rails themselves. Fresh underseal on an old truck deserves suspicion, not comfort.
2. **Originality of the drivetrain.** An F or 2F petrol six under the bonnet of an FJ is what the badge promises. Engine swaps (Lexus V8s, diesels into petrol trucks) are common in SA — they can make a great driver, but they cap the collector value.
3. **Paperwork.** Current registration in the seller's name, and if the truck came in from Namibia, Botswana or Zimbabwe — many did — the import paperwork. No papers, no deal, whatever the price.
4. **History and provenance.** A file of invoices, old photos, and a known ownership chain adds real money. This is what separates the R900k truck from the R450k one.
5. **Drive it.** These are simple machines: it should start easily, run cool, shift without crunching and track straight. Anything else is a negotiation item or a warning.
