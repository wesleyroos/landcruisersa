You have found the Land Cruiser. The photos are good, the kays are honest, and the price is... well, that is the question, isn't it? Is it a fair price? Is it negotiable? What does this thing *actually* sell for?

It is the most important question a buyer asks, and the strange truth is that almost nobody answers it honestly. So we went looking — through the economics literature, the South African pricing indices, and the data the big valuation houses publish — to separate what is actually known from what gets repeated on car-buying blogs. Here is what holds up.

## An asking price is a starting line, not a finish line

This is the first thing to internalise: **a listed price is where a negotiation begins.** It reflects what a seller hopes to get, anchored by what they paid, what they owe, and how they feel about their car — none of which is what it is worth to you.

The economics backs this up cleanly. A study of the US used-car retail market (Huang, *Journal of Economics & Management Strategy*, 2020) shows that listed prices carry an "unobserved discount" — a gap between the posted number and the price the car actually changes hands for — that can be statistically recovered from the data. Crucially, the size of that gap is **not uniform**: it depends on the seller's position. A dealer with strong demand and a rare model can afford to hold firm; a private seller who has already bought their next car cannot.

So the real question is never "what is the discount?" It is "how much room does *this* seller, on *this* car, actually have?"

## The gap is real — and dealers are better at the game than you

If you are buying from a dealer, know who you are up against. Research using administrative vehicle-transaction data (NBER working paper 34136, 2025) found that dealers extract **20–50% more surplus per transaction** than private sellers do — partly through better bargaining, partly by buying the right cars at the right prices in the first place.

And negotiation itself is genuinely imperfect. A landmark study of real bargaining sequences (Larsen, *Review of Economic Studies*, 2021) found that **17–24% of buyer–seller pairs failed to agree even when a deal would have made both better off.** (That particular study looked at wholesale dealer-to-dealer auctions rather than retail forecourts, so treat the exact figures as illustrative — but the lesson travels: deals fall apart, leverage matters, and information is power.)

The takeaway for you: the gap between asking and selling is real, but it is earned at the negotiating table, not assumed.

## The reference points you *should* be using in South Africa

Here is where most buyers go wrong — they treat the asking price as the reference point. It is not. South Africa has better ones.

**Trade value vs retail value.** Established providers like **TransUnion** and **Lightstone Auto** publish two numbers for any vehicle: a *trade* value (roughly what a dealer will pay you) and a *retail* value (roughly what a buyer should pay). The spread between them in South Africa is commonly cited at around **10–20%**. That spread is the closest thing the market has to a published "what it's worth to a seller vs a buyer" gap — and it is a far better anchor than the number on the windscreen.

**The Vehicle Pricing Index.** TransUnion's VPI tracks how used-vehicle prices are moving overall. In late 2024, used-vehicle price inflation was actually *negative* (−2.8% year on year) while new-car prices still rose — a sign of a softening, buyer-friendlier used market. (One honest caveat: the VPI measures price *levels over time*, not the discount off any single asking price. It tells you which way the wind is blowing, not what to offer.)

These providers do not pull their numbers from asking prices. TransUnion states it models "realistic retail" from data on tens of thousands of actual vehicles a month; Lightstone draws on millions of recorded transactions. That is the point: **the people who value cars seriously do not rely on what sellers are asking.** Neither should you.

## What a price drop actually means

We track price changes on every listing, so we have to be honest about what they do and don't tell you.

A price drop is a real, useful signal — it usually means a seller who has accepted the market is moving, and who may have more room to move. Buyers and pricing tools alike treat a listing's age and its price reductions as leverage.

But there is a confounder you must hold in mind: **a car that has sat for a long time and dropped in price is not automatically a bargain.** It can equally be a vehicle that was overpriced from day one, or one that is simply hard to sell. A price cut tells you a seller is motivated. It does not tell you the car is worth buying. Use it as an opening, not a verdict.

(You will sometimes see confident claims online like "wait exactly 30 days and you'll get 5–15% off." We checked those against their sources. They do not hold up — treat them as folklore, not fact.)

## The number nobody in South Africa publishes

Here is the gap in all of this, and it is a big one: **no public South African source links what a vehicle was *listed* at to what it actually *sold* for.** Not TransUnion, not the banks, not the big portals. The valuation houses model retail value, the indices track inflation, the blogs guess — but the simple, specific number every buyer wants ("what do these actually go for versus the ask?") is not published anywhere. For 4x4s, SUVs and bakkies specifically, there is essentially no segment-level data on it at all.

## What this means for your next Land Cruiser

1. **Treat the asking price as an opening offer.** It reflects the seller's hopes, not the car's worth.
2. **Anchor on trade and retail values**, not the windscreen. Pull both from a real valuation provider and compare.
3. **Compare across the whole market, not one listing.** A single price means nothing without the spread of similar vehicles around it — which is exactly what our [live market data](/market/) pages show.
4. **Read price history as leverage, not proof.** A drop opens a door; it is your homework that tells you whether to walk through it.
5. **Be wary of anyone quoting a precise "discount %."** The honest answer is that it depends on the seller, the model, and the moment — and in South Africa, the clean number simply isn't published. Yet.

Asking prices start the conversation. Everything that matters happens after.
