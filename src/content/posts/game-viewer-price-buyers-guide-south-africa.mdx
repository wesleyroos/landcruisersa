## What is a game viewer?

A game viewer is an **open-sided safari vehicle with raised, tiered bench seating** — the vehicle that carries guests on a game drive at a lodge or private reserve. Most seat nine to eleven passengers on stepped rows behind the driver and guide, giving everyone a clear, elevated view over the bush.

In South Africa the overwhelming majority are built on a **[Toyota Land Cruiser 79](/listings/model/79-series/) single cab** — the 4.2 diesel or 2.8 GD-6 pick-up — because its ladder-frame chassis, solid axles and reputation for durability suit years of slow, heavy, low-range work. The **[78 Series Troopcarrier](/listings/model/78-series/)** is also used, as are the FJ and Prado for lighter or private duty. The one factory-built exception is the Mahindra Pik Up S4 Game Viewer, covered below.

## What does a game viewer cost? The three routes

There is no single price because there are three different ways to end up with a game viewer, and they sit at very different price points:

| Route | Typical price | What you get |
|---|---|---|
| **Buy a used converted one** | ~R189,950 – R1,599,900 (median ~R749,900) | A ready-to-drive game viewer; older/high-hours at the bottom, near-new turnkey at the top |
| **Convert a vehicle you own** | R95,000 – R175,000 *(plus the donor vehicle)* | The conversion work only — roof cut, tiered seating, safety doors, canvas, paint |
| **Buy factory-new (Mahindra S4)** | R521,599 (4×2) / R565,999 (4×4) | A brand-new, warrantied factory game viewer, nine rear seats |

The number most buyers miss is in the middle row. A new turnkey Land Cruiser 79 game viewer is listed at R1.3 million or more **because that price includes a brand-new Land Cruiser** — the conversion work itself is only **R95,000 for a standard three-row conversion, or R175,000 for a larger five-row truck conversion** (roof cut, bench seating, ten safety doors and steps, canvas upholstery and paint). If you already own a suitable Cruiser, or buy a good used one, converting it is dramatically cheaper than buying a finished example. If you don't, you're paying for the donor either way.

## Land Cruiser vs Mahindra game viewer

For years a game viewer effectively *meant* a converted Land Cruiser. Mahindra changed that with a **factory-built Pik Up S4 Game Viewer**, sold new through franchised dealers from **about R521,599 (4×2) or R565,999 (4×4)**, seating nine in separated rear bucket seats plus the driver and guide. It arrives as a complete, warrantied vehicle — no separate conversion step.

![The Mahindra Pik Up S4 Game Viewer — a factory-built game viewer sold new in South Africa from about R521,599](https://pub-6c900fb2e73a4b89bc049099101e4591.r2.dev/images/posts/mahindra-s4-game-viewer.jpg)

That makes the decision a genuine fork:

- **Mahindra S4 Game Viewer** — far lower upfront cost, factory warranty, brand-new. The trade-off is a smaller aftermarket, less proven longevity under sustained safari use, and weaker resale.
- **Land Cruiser 79 conversion** — much more expensive (a new turnkey example runs past R1.3 million), but you buy the 79's proven durability, near-universal parts availability in remote areas, and the strongest resale in the segment. This is why established lodges and reserves still default to it despite the price.

Put simply: choose the Mahindra if upfront budget is the priority, and the Land Cruiser if you need a vehicle that will absorb a decade of hard use and hold its value.

## What to check before you buy a used game viewer

A game viewer has led a harder life than its odometer suggests, so the usual used-car checklist shifts:

1. **Hours, not just kilometres.** Game-drive kilometres are slow, dusty and heavy on the drivetrain. A low odometer reading can hide thousands of hours of low-range idling and load. Ask about service history and how the vehicle was used, and treat a full record as more valuable than a low number.
2. **Lodge fleet retirements are the value buy.** Many of the best-priced used game viewers come from lodges refreshing their fleet. These are often well-maintained on a strict schedule — ask for the maintenance records.
3. **Is the conversion on the licence and roadworthy?** The open body and added seats are a modification (see below). Confirm the vehicle is correctly registered for its current configuration and can pass a roadworthy test as a game viewer, not just as its original pick-up.
4. **Condition of the conversion itself.** Check the bench frames, floor and safety doors for corrosion, and the canvas and seating for sun and wear.

## Is a game viewer road-legal?

Converting a pick-up into a game viewer changes the body and the number of seats, and under South African law **altering the number of passenger seats is a modification** that must comply with the national compulsory vehicle specifications administered by the NRCS. In practice that means a legitimate game viewer must be correctly registered for its converted configuration and able to pass a roadworthy test in that form. When buying, confirm the paperwork matches the vehicle in front of you.

If you intend to **carry paying passengers commercially** — the situation for any lodge or safari operator — there is a second layer:

- an **operating licence** from the National Public Transport Regulator (NPTR), required for tourist transport services;
- a driver holding a **professional driving permit (PrDP)**;
- **public passenger liability insurance**; and
- critically for used buyers, the vehicle must have been **first registered in 2009 or later** to qualify for a *new* operating licence.

That last point matters: an older, pre-2009 game viewer is perfectly fine for private or farm use, but **cannot be put onto a new commercial operating licence** — worth knowing before you buy one intending to run paid drives.

> This section is general guidance, not legal advice. Registration, roadworthy and operating-licence requirements change and are enforced by the NRCS and the NPTR (and your provincial regulator). Confirm the current rules with them, and with a qualified professional, before you buy or operate.
